The Manila Cathedral

Minor Basilica of the Immaculate Conception

     The Manila Cathedral, formally known as the Minor Basilica and Metropolitan Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception, stands as the mother church of the Philippines. Located in Intramuros, it symbolizes the country’s deep Catholic faith, the spanish colonial history, and architectural resilience.

History

     First built in 1571, the Manila Cathedral has been reconstructed eight times due to earthquakes, fires, and wars. In 1579, Pope Gregory XIII established Manila as a diocese under the Archdiocese of Mexico, elevating its religious importance.

     The current structure, completed in 1958, reflects the perseverance of the Filipino people. In 1981, Pope John Paul II granted it the title of a minor basilica. Since then, it has been visited by three popes: Paul VI, John Paul II, and Pope Francis. The present Manila Cathedral rose phoenix-like from the desolate ruins of the old cathedral, which was bombed mercilessly along with the rest of the Walled City during the Battle of Liberation in 1945. Asserting back its distinction and dignity as Manila’s premier temple and metropolitan seat of the Archdiocese, the present structure emerged in the midst of the pathetic remnants of Intramuros, its open spaces colonized by squatters and its ancient ruins converted into cargo warehouses.

     For many years after the war, the shell of the Manila Cathedral stood before the gaze of the statue of Carlos IV in Plaza Roma. The ruins of its famous façade remained. There was the rose window without the colored glass and the three portals with their receding arches and the cathedral’s historical marker.

Architecture

Built in a Neo-Romanesque style, the cathedral follows a Latin cross layout. Its grand façade is adorned with statues of saints sculpted from Roman travertine stone. The massive bronze doors, stained glass windows, and detailed arches showcase European craftsmanship blended with Filipino devotion.
